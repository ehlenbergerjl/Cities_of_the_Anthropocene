Cities of the Anthropocene

Cities of the Anthropocene

Are cities the reckless climate change drivers of the Anthropocene? Can they turn the wheel and drive humankind in a new, more sustainable direction, or will our survival come down to Deus ex Machina?

Grades

8 - 9

Subjects

Anthropology, Climatology, Physical Geography, Human Geography, Urban Planning

Though climate scientists have been warning of the dire consequences of unabated human consumption of Earth's resources since the mid-twentieth century, there's no way we could have known at the beginning of the industrial revolution that the building of the new world would ultimately lead to the end of humankind's presence in it. Stephanie Pincetl, a Professor at the UCLA Institute of the Environment and Sustainability and Founding Director of the California Center for Sustainable Communities at UCLA, has written extensively about climate change. In her article, Cities in the age of the Anthropocene: Climate change agents and the potential for mitigation, she looked at the climate crisis through the lens of cities as drivers of destructive climate change and how it must be cities that turn it around1. The article predates the COVID-19 pandemic by a few years, but it's hard not to notice what an effect a radical reduction of emissions can have on a city's skyline.

The World Urban Forum 10 concluded that there are around 10,000 cities worldwide. That's up from about 5,000 in 1980. If you can image 10,000 skylines clearing by that much in only a few months, you have an idea of how a radical reduction in fossil fuels consumption really can make a difference over the long term. It won't be a silver bullet. Although cities cover only 2 percent of the worlds land surface, they consume over 75% of Earths material resources" (UNEP, 2013)

According to climate scientists, if the world stopped using fossil fuels completely, today, the earth would continue to warm for centuries to come, albeit far less than more realistic projections. In fact, climate scientists don't even refer to a reversal in global warming as an option - they refer to a radical reduction in greenhouse gasses (GHG), such as CO2, methane, and other trait gases, as 'bending the curve'2. To really make a difference, there must be a cross-sector effort to enact innovative solutions. You can play with the numbers, yourself, using MIT's En-ROADS Simulator.

The global economy has driven urbanization, especially with the advent of mass agriculture. People are migrating to cities for jobs, and they're bringing the family station wagon. People are moving to cities from their hinterlands, seeking economic prosperity as a function well-being. Unfortunately, cities and the economic forces that enable their rise are heavily dependent on cheap fossil fuels. The more we use fossil fuels, the more we impact the environment.

Pincetl argues that economic growth must be decoupled from the sense of 'well-being', and that a new ethics of repair, nourishing humanities sense of mindfulness of each other and of the Earth we call home. The goal is educating the next generation, the most important generation, on the multigenerational implications of unabated climate change and plausible, attainable actions that can be made now, and in the near future to bend the curve, and save humanity.

Anthropocene
Noun

period of time during which human activities have impacted the environment enough to constitute a distinct geological change.
atmosphere
Noun

layers of gases surrounding a planet or other celestial body.
climate
Noun

all weather conditions for a given location over a period of time.
ecosystem
Noun

community and interactions of living and nonliving things in an area.
geologic time scale
Noun

system to date the events that have occurred during the history of Earth, based on the composition of rock layers.
Holocene
Noun

having to do with the present geological time period. The Holocene Epoch began at the end of the last glacial period, about 10,000 years ago.
Industrial Revolution
Noun

change in economic and social activities, beginning in the 18th century, brought by the replacement of hand tools with machinery and mass production.
radioactive
Adjective

having unstable atomic nuclei and emitting subatomic particles and radiation.
strata
Plural Noun

(singular: stratum) layers of rock or other materials.
stratigraphy
Noun

study of rock layers and layering.

      Extinction is the complete disappearance of a species from Earth. Species go extinct every year, but historically the average rate of extinction has been very slow with a few exceptions. The fossil record reveals five uniquely large mass extinction events during which significant events such as asteroid strikes and volcanic eruptions caused widespread extinctions over relatively short periods of time. Some scientists think we might have entered our sixth mass extinction event driven largely by human activity. Our planet is dependent on an interconnected system. If we lose one species, how does that impact the whole system? What if we lose hundreds? Help your students understand the gravity of extinction with these classroom resources.
